Volt
The volt (symbol: V) is the SI unit of electric potential difference, electric potential, and electromotive force. It describes how much energy is available to move electric charge.
V

Definition

One volt is defined as the electric potential difference between two points when one joule of energy is transferred per coulomb of electric charge.
1 V = 1 J / 1 C

Physical Meaning

Voltage represents electrical “pressure” that pushes electric charges through a circuit. A higher voltage means more energy is available to drive current.

While voltage enables current flow, current only flows when a complete circuit exists and resistance allows it.

Relation to Other Electrical Quantities

Voltage is directly related to current and resistance through Ohm's Law.

V = I × R

Where I is current in amperes and R is resistance in ohms.

Measurement

Voltage is measured using a voltmeter or a digital multimeter.

Voltmeters are connected in parallel with the component or section of the circuit being measured.

AC and DC Voltage

Direct Current (DC)

DC voltage has a constant polarity and magnitude, commonly supplied by batteries and power supplies.

Alternating Current (AC)

AC voltage periodically reverses direction and varies in magnitude, as used in household and industrial power systems.

Common Voltage Levels

  • 1.5 V — standard AA/AAA battery
  • 5 V — USB power
  • 12 V — automotive electrical systems
  • 120 V / 230 V — household mains (region-dependent)

Historical Background

The volt is named after Alessandro Volta, an Italian physicist who invented the first electric battery, the voltaic pile.

The unit was officially adopted as part of the International System of Units (SI) in the 20th century.

Safety Considerations

High voltages can pose serious safety hazards, including electric shock and arcing.

Safety standards classify voltage levels and environments to reduce risk when working with electrical systems.
