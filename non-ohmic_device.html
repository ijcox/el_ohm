Non-Ohmic Devices
Non-ohmic devices are electrical components whose voltage–current relationship does not follow Ohm's Law, meaning their resistance is not constant.
V ≠ IR

What Makes a Device Non-Ohmic?

A device is considered non-ohmic when the ratio of voltage to current changes as operating conditions vary.

This usually occurs because the material properties, temperature, or internal electric fields change with applied voltage or current.

Voltage–Current Characteristics

Non-ohmic devices exhibit a nonlinear current–voltage (I–V) curve rather than a straight line.

Resistance = V / I (not constant)

Because resistance varies, a single resistance value in ohms cannot fully describe the device.

Common Examples

Semiconductor Devices

  • Diodes — conduct primarily in one direction
  • Transistors — current controlled by voltage or current at another terminal
  • Light-emitting diodes (LEDs)

Temperature-Dependent Devices

  • Thermistors (NTC and PTC)
  • Incandescent lamp filaments

Other Examples

  • Electrolytic cells
  • Gas discharge tubes
  • Varistors

Why Ohm's Law Fails

Ohm's Law assumes a constant resistance, which is only valid for ohmic materials.

In non-ohmic devices, resistance may change due to:

  • Temperature rise caused by current flow
  • Electric field–dependent carrier mobility
  • Charge carrier generation or recombination
  • Material phase changes

Graphical Interpretation

On a voltage–current graph:

  • Ohmic devices produce a straight line through the origin
  • Non-ohmic devices produce curved or segmented plots

The slope of the curve at any point represents the dynamic (differential) resistance.

Engineering Importance

Non-ohmic devices form the foundation of modern electronics, enabling:

  • Signal amplification
  • Rectification and switching
  • Voltage regulation
  • Sensing and control systems
