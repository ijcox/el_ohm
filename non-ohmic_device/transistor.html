Transistor
A transistor is a semiconductor device used to amplify or switch electronic signals and electrical power. It is a fundamental building block of modern electronic systems.
Q

Purpose and Function

Transistors control the flow of current using a small input signal, enabling signal amplification, digital logic, and power control.

Basic Operating Principle

A transistor uses semiconductor junctions to regulate current flow. A small change at one terminal controls a much larger current between the other terminals.

This behavior distinguishes transistors from passive components like resistors and diodes.

Main Types of Transistors

Bipolar Junction Transistor (BJT)

BJTs use both electrons and holes as charge carriers and have three terminals: emitter, base, and collector.

IC ≈ β × IB

Field-Effect Transistor (FET)

FETs control current using an electric field. Common types include MOSFETs and JFETs.

FETs are voltage-controlled devices with very high input impedance.

Operating Regions

  • Cutoff — transistor is off
  • Active — amplification region
  • Saturation — fully on (switching)

Transistors as Amplifiers

In amplification, a small input signal controls a larger output signal, increasing voltage, current, or power.

Amplifier behavior is often analyzed using Ohm's Law and small-signal models.

Transistors as Switches

When used as switches, transistors rapidly alternate between cutoff and saturation states.

This switching capability forms the basis of digital logic and computing.

Applications

  • Microprocessors and memory
  • Signal amplification
  • Power regulation
  • Motor control
  • Wireless communication

Historical Background

The transistor was invented in 1947 at Bell Labs, revolutionizing electronics by replacing bulky and inefficient vacuum tubes.

Modern integrated circuits contain billions of transistors on a single chip.

Testing and Measurement

Transistors are commonly tested using a digital multimeter, curve tracer, or specialized semiconductor tester.
