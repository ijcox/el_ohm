Light-Emitting Diodes (LEDs)

Symbol: ➤|

What Is an LED?

A light-emitting diode (LED) is a semiconductor device that emits light when current flows through it. LEDs are a specialized type of diode designed to convert electrical energy directly into visible or invisible light.

LEDs are widely used in indicators, displays, lighting systems, communication devices, and optical sensing. Unlike incandescent bulbs, LEDs are highly energy-efficient, durable, and compact.

How LEDs Work

LEDs operate using the principle of electroluminescence. When a forward voltage is applied across the p-n junction, electrons recombine with holes in the semiconductor material. This recombination releases energy in the form of photons (light).

The color of light depends on the semiconductor material and its bandgap energy.

Energy of photon (E) = h × f

Where h is Planck's constant and f is the frequency of emitted light.

Electrical Characteristics

LEDs are non-ohmic devices. Their current-voltage relationship is nonlinear.

  • Forward Voltage (VF): Typically 1.8V–3.6V depending on color.
  • Forward Current: Usually 10–30 mA for standard LEDs.
  • Reverse Voltage: Low tolerance; excessive reverse voltage can damage the LED.

Because LEDs do not obey Ohm's Law directly, they must be used with a current-limiting resistor.

R = (Vsource − VLED) / I

Types of LEDs

  • Standard Indicator LEDs – Small signal lights.
  • High-Power LEDs – Used in lighting applications.
  • RGB LEDs – Combine red, green, and blue in one package.
  • Infrared LEDs – Used in remote controls and sensors.
  • OLEDs – Organic LEDs used in displays.
  • Surface-Mount LEDs (SMD) – Compact, for PCB mounting.

Efficiency and Advantages

  • High energy efficiency
  • Long lifespan (25,000–100,000 hours)
  • Low heat generation
  • Shock resistance
  • Fast switching speed

Applications

  • Indicator lights in electronics
  • Digital displays
  • Automotive lighting
  • Traffic signals
  • Room and industrial lighting
  • Optical communication systems

Comparison with Other Devices

LEDs are often compared with other semiconductor devices such as diodes, transistors, and thermistors. While standard diodes regulate current flow, LEDs convert electrical energy into visible radiation.

Measuring LEDs

LEDs can be tested using a digital multimeter in diode test mode. This mode applies a small forward voltage and displays the forward voltage drop.

For accurate voltage and current measurements, use instruments measuring in volts and amperes.
