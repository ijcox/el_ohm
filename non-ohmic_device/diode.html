Diode
A diode is a two-terminal semiconductor device that allows electric current to flow primarily in one direction while blocking it in the opposite direction.
▶|

Basic Function

A diode acts as an electrical one-way valve. When forward-biased, it conducts current; when reverse-biased, it largely prevents current flow.

Semiconductor Structure

Most diodes are made from a p–n junction, formed by joining p-type and n-type semiconductor materials.

The junction creates a depletion region that controls current flow based on applied voltage.

Forward and Reverse Bias

Forward Bias

When the anode is at a higher potential than the cathode, the diode conducts current after exceeding its forward voltage drop.

Reverse Bias

When the polarity is reversed, the diode blocks current except for a very small leakage current.

Voltage–Current Relationship

Diodes are non-ohmic devices, meaning their current does not increase linearly with voltage.

I ≈ Iₛ (eV / nVT − 1)

This exponential behavior distinguishes diodes from resistive components.

Common Types of Diodes

  • Rectifier diode — AC to DC conversion
  • Zener diode — voltage regulation
  • Light-emitting diode (LED) — light generation
  • Schottky diode — low forward voltage drop
  • Photodiode — light detection

Typical Applications

Diodes are used extensively in power supplies, signal processing, protection circuits, and digital electronics.

  • Rectification in power supplies
  • Reverse-polarity protection
  • Signal demodulation
  • Voltage reference circuits

Diodes in Circuits

In practical circuits, diode behavior is often analyzed using Ohm's Law alongside diode characteristic curves.

Measurement and testing are commonly performed with a digital multimeter using diode-test mode.

Historical Background

Early diodes were vacuum-tube devices, later replaced by semiconductor diodes as solid-state electronics advanced in the mid-20th century.

Modern diodes are fundamental building blocks of integrated circuits.
