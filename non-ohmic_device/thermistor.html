Thermistor
A thermistor is a temperature-sensitive resistor whose resistance changes significantly with temperature. It is widely used for temperature sensing, control, and circuit protection.
R(T)

Basic Principle

Thermistors are made from semiconductor materials whose resistance varies predictably with temperature.

Unlike ordinary resistors, thermistors are non-ohmic devices, meaning their resistance does not remain constant.

Main Types

NTC (Negative Temperature Coefficient)

Resistance decreases as temperature increases.

PTC (Positive Temperature Coefficient)

Resistance increases as temperature increases.

Resistance–Temperature Relationship

The resistance of an NTC thermistor is commonly approximated by:

R = R₀ · eβ(1/T − 1/T₀)

Where R is resistance at temperature T (in kelvin), and β is a material constant.

Applications

  • Temperature measurement and monitoring
  • Battery pack temperature sensing
  • Inrush current limiting (NTC)
  • Overcurrent protection (PTC resettable fuses)
  • HVAC and appliance control systems

Thermistors in Circuits

Thermistors are often used in voltage divider circuits to convert temperature changes into measurable voltage changes.

Circuit analysis typically uses Ohm's Law along with the thermistor’s temperature-resistance curve.

Advantages and Limitations

Advantages

  • High sensitivity
  • Low cost
  • Small size

Limitations

  • Non-linear response
  • Limited temperature range
  • Self-heating effects

Measurement and Testing

Thermistors can be tested using a digital multimeter by measuring resistance and comparing it to expected values at a known temperature.

Comparison with Other Sensors

Thermistors differ from RTDs and thermocouples in response curve, sensitivity, and operating range.
