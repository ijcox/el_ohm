Photodiodes

Symbol: ➤| ⇢⇢ (light arrows)

Overview

A photodiode is a light-sensitive semiconductor device that converts light into electrical current.

It is a specialized type of diode designed to operate primarily in reverse bias.

How It Works

When photons strike the p-n junction of the photodiode, they generate electron-hole pairs. This creates a photocurrent proportional to the intensity of the incident light.

I_total = I_dark + I_ph

Where:

  • I_dark = small current present without light
  • I_ph = current generated by light

Photodiodes are considered non-ohmic devices and do not obey Ohm's Law in a linear manner.

Modes of Operation

Photoconductive Mode
Reverse-biased operation for fast response.
Photovoltaic Mode
Operates with zero bias, like a solar cell.
Avalanche Mode
High reverse voltage provides internal gain.

Key Characteristics

  • Responsivity: Output current per unit optical power (A/W)
  • Dark Current: Leakage current in absence of light
  • Response Time: Speed of detection
  • Spectral Response: Sensitivity to wavelength

Types of Photodiodes

PN Photodiode
Standard junction structure
PIN Photodiode
Intrinsic layer improves sensitivity and speed
Avalanche Photodiode (APD)
Internal signal amplification

Applications

  • Light sensors
  • Optical communication systems
  • Fiber optic receivers
  • Medical instruments
  • Remote controls
  • Safety and intrusion detection systems

Measurement and Testing

Photodiodes can be tested using a digital multimeter to measure voltage or current response under illumination.

Voltage measurements are expressed in volts, and photocurrent is measured in amperes.
