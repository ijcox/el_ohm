Ohmmeter
An ohmmeter is an electrical measuring instrument used to determine the resistance of a component or circuit, expressed in ohms.
Ω

Purpose and Function

An ohmmeter measures how strongly a component resists the flow of electric current. It is commonly used to test resistors, verify circuit continuity, and diagnose faults such as open or short circuits.

How an Ohmmeter Works

An ohmmeter operates by applying a small internal voltage to the component under test and measuring the resulting current.

Resistance = V / I

Using the principles of Ohm's Law, the instrument calculates resistance based on the measured current.

Types of Ohmmeters

Series Ohmmeter

Uses an internal battery and measures current through the unknown resistance. Common in analog meters.

Shunt Ohmmeter

Places the unknown resistance in parallel and is useful for measuring low resistances.

Digital Ohmmeter

Found in modern digital multimeters, offering high accuracy, auto-ranging, and digital displays.

Measurement Ranges

Ohmmeters typically support multiple ranges, from milliohms (mΩ) to megaohms (MΩ), allowing measurement of both conductors and insulators.

  • Low resistance: wires, contacts
  • Medium resistance: discrete resistors
  • High resistance: insulation, leakage paths

Proper Usage

To obtain accurate readings, the circuit under test must be de-energized.

  • Disconnect power sources before measurement
  • Discharge capacitors
  • Isolate the component if possible

Limitations

Ohmmeters assume linear resistance behavior and may give misleading results when used on non-ohmic devices such as diodes or transistors.

Temperature changes and contact resistance can also affect accuracy.

Historical Development

Early ohmmeters were analog instruments developed alongside the formal definition of the ohm in the 19th century.

Modern digital ohmmeters use precision analog-to-digital converters and microcontrollers for improved accuracy and usability.
