Rectifier Diode

Symbol: ➤|

Overview

A rectifier diode is a semiconductor device designed specifically for converting alternating current (AC) into direct current (DC). It is one of the most important types of diodes used in power electronics.

Rectifier diodes are optimized to handle relatively high currents and voltages compared to small signal diodes.

How Rectification Works

Rectification is the process of converting AC to DC by allowing current to pass during only one portion of the AC waveform.

During the positive half-cycle, the diode is forward-biased and conducts. During the negative half-cycle, it is reverse-biased and blocks current.

Types of Rectifier Circuits

Half-Wave Rectifier
Uses one diode to pass only one half of the AC waveform.
Full-Wave Rectifier
Uses two diodes with a center-tapped transformer.
Bridge Rectifier
Uses four diodes to convert both halves of AC without a center tap.

Electrical Characteristics

  • Forward Voltage Drop: ~0.7V (silicon)
  • Maximum Forward Current (IF): Rated continuous current
  • Peak Inverse Voltage (PIV): Maximum reverse voltage
  • Reverse Recovery Time: Time to switch from conducting to blocking
PIV ≥ V_peak (AC source)

Rectifier diodes are non-ohmic devices and do not follow a simple linear resistance model from Ohm's Law.

Filtering and Smoothing

After rectification, the output contains ripple. Capacitors are commonly added to smooth the DC output.

V_ripple ≈ I_load / (f × C)

Where:

  • I_load = Load current
  • f = Ripple frequency
  • C = Filter capacitance

Applications

  • Power supplies
  • Battery chargers
  • DC motor drives
  • HVDC transmission systems
  • Industrial equipment

Measurement and Testing

Rectifier diodes can be tested using an ohmmeter or digital multimeter in diode mode.

Forward voltage is measured in volts, and current handling capability is rated in amperes.
