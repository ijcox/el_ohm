Ohm's Law
Ohm's Law defines the fundamental relationship between voltage, current, and resistance in electrical circuits and serves as one of the core principles of electronics and electrical engineering.
V = IR

Statement of the Law

Ohm's Law states that the electric current flowing through a conductor is directly proportional to the voltage across it, provided the temperature and physical conditions remain constant.
V = I × R
This relationship allows any one of the three quantities to be calculated when the other two are known.

Variables Explained

  • Voltage (V) — electrical potential difference, measured in volts
  • Current (I) — rate of electric charge flow, measured in amperes
  • Resistance (R) — opposition to current flow, measured in ohms

Alternative Forms

Ohm's Law can be rearranged algebraically to solve for different variables:

I = V / R
R = V / I

Physical Interpretation

Ohm's Law describes how electric charge carriers respond to an applied electric field within a material.

A higher voltage increases the force on charges, increasing current, while higher resistance limits charge motion due to material structure, impurities, and temperature effects.

Conditions and Limitations

Ohm's Law applies accurately to ohmic materials, where resistance remains constant over a wide range of voltages and currents.

Many real-world components do not strictly follow Ohm's Law, including:

  • Diodes and transistors
  • Incandescent lamps
  • Electrolytes and gas discharge tubes

These are examples of non-ohmic devices.

Relation to Electrical Power

Combined with power equations, Ohm's Law allows calculation of energy dissipation in resistive circuits:

P = V × I
P = I² × R

Historical Background

Ohm's Law is named after German physicist Georg Simon Ohm (1789–1854), who published his findings in 1827 after extensive experimentation with electrical circuits.

Although initially criticized, the law became a cornerstone of classical circuit theory and modern electronics.
