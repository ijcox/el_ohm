Ampere
The ampere (symbol: A) is the SI unit of electric current. It measures the rate at which electric charge flows through a conductor.
A

Definition

One ampere is defined as the flow of one coulomb of electric charge per second.
1 A = 1 C / 1 s

Physical Meaning

Electric current describes how quickly electric charges move through a material.

While voltage provides the driving force, current represents the actual movement of charge carriers such as electrons.

Relation to Other Electrical Quantities

Current, voltage, and resistance are related through Ohm's Law.

I = V / R

Where V is voltage in volts and R is resistance in ohms.

Measurement

Current is measured using an ammeter or a digital multimeter.

Unlike voltage measurement, current measurement requires the meter to be placed in series with the circuit.

AC and DC Current

Direct Current (DC)

DC current flows in a single direction and is typical of battery-powered systems.

Alternating Current (AC)

AC current periodically reverses direction, commonly used in power distribution systems.

Typical Current Levels

  • Microamperes (µA) — sensors and low-power electronics
  • Milliamperes (mA) — small electronic devices
  • Amperes (A) — household appliances
  • Kiloamperes (kA) — industrial and power systems

Historical Background

The ampere is named after André-Marie Ampère, a pioneer in the study of electromagnetism.

The modern SI definition is based on fixed values of fundamental physical constants.

Safety Considerations

High currents can generate heat, cause burns, or start fires.

Circuit protection devices such as fuses and circuit breakers limit current to safe levels.
