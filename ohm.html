Ohm
The ohm is the SI derived unit of electrical resistance, describing how strongly a material, component, or circuit opposes the flow of electric current.
Ω

Formal Definition

One ohm is defined as the electrical resistance between two points of a conductor when a constant potential difference of one volt results in a current of one ampere.
1 Ω = 1 V / 1 A
This definition directly follows from Ohm's Law, which governs the behavior of ideal resistive elements.

Mathematical Relationships

The ohm connects voltage, current, power, and energy dissipation within electrical systems.

V = I × R
  • Resistance: R (ohms, Ω)
  • Voltage: V (volts)
  • Current: I (amperes)

Combined with power equations, resistance also determines heat generation in a conductor:

P = I² × R

Physical Meaning of Resistance

Resistance measured in ohms represents how charge carriers (typically electrons) interact with the atomic structure of a material.

In metals, resistance arises primarily from electron scattering due to lattice vibrations and impurities. In semiconductors and insulators, the band structure plays a dominant role.

Low vs. High Resistance

  • Low resistance (milliohms to a few ohms): wires, bus bars
  • Moderate resistance (tens to thousands of ohms): resistors
  • High resistance (megaohms and above): insulators, leakage paths

Equivalent Resistances

When multiple resistive elements are combined, the resulting resistance in ohms depends on their configuration.

Series Connection

Rtotal = R₁ + R₂ + …

Parallel Connection

1 / Rtotal = 1 / R₁ + 1 / R₂ + …

Measurement of Ohms

Resistance is commonly measured using an ohmmeter, a function found in most digital multimeters.

Accurate measurement requires the circuit to be de-energized, as external voltages can distort resistance readings.

Historical Background

The ohm is named after German physicist Georg Simon Ohm (1789–1854), who published his findings on electrical conduction in 1827.

Although initially controversial, Ohm’s work became foundational to circuit theory and modern electrical engineering.
